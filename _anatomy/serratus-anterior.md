# Serratus Anterior

The serratus anterior is a fan-shaped muscle on the side of the ribs that anchors the scapula to the ribcage. It's essential for pushing movements and overhead reach.

## Anatomy

### Shape and Location

**Fan or finger-like:**
- Wraps around ribcage
- Visible in lean individuals
- "Serrated" appearance

### Attachments

**Origin:**
- Ribs 1-8 (or 9)
- Lateral rib surfaces

**Insertion:**
- Medial border of scapula
- Anterior surface (front)

## Function

### Primary Actions

**Protraction:**
- Pushes scapula forward
- Punching motion
- "Boxer's muscle"

**Upward rotation:**
- Rotates scapula for overhead reach
- Works with trapezius

**Holds scapula to ribs:**
- Prevents winging
- Foundation for arm movement

## Importance

### Scapular Stability

**Without serratus:**
- Scapula wings off ribcage
- Arm function impaired
- Shoulder problems develop

### Overhead Function

**For reaching up:**
- Upward rotation required
- Serratus + lower/upper trap
- Coordinated action

## Problems

### Weakness

**Causes:**
- Disuse
- Nerve injury (long thoracic)
- Poor training

**Results:**
- Scapular winging
- Shoulder dysfunction
- Limited overhead reach

### Scapular Winging

**Visible sign:**
- Medial border lifts off ribs
- During pushing or reaching
- May be neurological or muscular
