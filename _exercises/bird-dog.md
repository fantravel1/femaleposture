# Bird Dog Exercise: Core Stability & Balance

> **At a Glance:** Bird dog is a fundamental core exercise that builds spinal stability while challenging balance and coordination. It strengthens the back, glutes, and core simultaneously while teaching your body to move limbs without compromising spinal position.

---

## Quick Facts

| | |
|---|---|
| **Targets** | Erector spinae, multifidus, glutes, core |
| **Best For** | Core stability, lower back pain, balance |
| **Difficulty** | Beginner |
| **Equipment** | Mat (optional) |
| **Time** | 3-5 minutes |
| **Frequency** | Daily |

---

## Why This Exercise Works

Modern life often creates:
- Weak deep stabilizing muscles
- Poor coordination between core and limbs
- Lower back that compensates for weak core
- Difficulty maintaining neutral spine during movement

Bird dog addresses all of these by:
- Training anti-rotation and anti-extension strength
- Building coordination between opposite arm and leg
- Strengthening the entire posterior chain
- Teaching spinal awareness and control
- Being low-impact and accessible

---

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Starting Position

1. Start on hands and knees (tabletop position)
2. Wrists directly under shoulders
3. Knees directly under hips
4. Spine in neutral (natural curves maintained)
5. Head in line with spine, gaze toward floor
6. Core gently engaged (not sucking in, just activated)

### The Movement

**Step 1:** Maintain a stable, neutral spine throughout

**Step 2:** Slowly extend your right arm forward (thumb up)

**Step 3:** Simultaneously extend your left leg backward

**Step 4:** Reach as if someone is gently pulling your hand and foot apart

**Step 5:** Keep hips level—don't let them rotate or drop

**Step 6:** Hold for 5-10 seconds while breathing normally

**Step 7:** Return arm and leg to starting position with control

**Step 8:** Repeat with left arm and right leg

**Step 9:** Alternate sides

### Breathing

- Breathe continuously—don't hold breath
- Exhale as you extend arm and leg
- Inhale as you return
- Keep breathing steady during holds

## What You Should Feel

### Good Sensations

- Glute working on the extended leg side
- Core muscles engaging to prevent rotation
- Back muscles supporting spine
- Challenge to maintain balance
- No movement or wobbling in the spine

### Red Flags—Stop If:

- Lower back pain
- Sharp pain anywhere
- Excessive shaking (may need easier version)
- Can't maintain neutral spine

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Arching the Lower Back

**What it looks like:** Lower back sags, belly drops toward floor
**Why it's wrong:** Loses core engagement; strains lower back
**How to fix:** Keep abs engaged; imagine balancing a cup of water on your back

### Mistake 2: Lifting Leg Too High

**What it looks like:** Leg raised above hip level, back arches
**Why it's wrong:** Creates compensation; not more effective
**How to fix:** Only lift leg to hip height; focus on length, not height

### Mistake 3: Rotating Hips

**What it looks like:** Hip of lifted leg rotates open toward ceiling
**Why it's wrong:** Loses anti-rotation benefit; easier but less effective
**How to fix:** Keep both hip bones pointing toward floor

### Mistake 4: Looking Up

**What it looks like:** Cranking neck back to look forward
**Why it's wrong:** Strains neck; changes spinal alignment
**How to fix:** Keep gaze down; neck as natural extension of spine

### Mistake 5: Moving Too Fast

**What it looks like:** Quick, jerky movements
**Why it's wrong:** Uses momentum; loses stability benefit
**How to fix:** Slow, controlled movement; 3-4 seconds each direction

## Modifications and Variations

### Making It Easier

**Arm Only:**
1. Keep both knees down
2. Only extend arm
3. Focus on keeping spine stable
4. Good starting point for beginners

**Leg Only:**
1. Keep both hands down
2. Only extend leg
3. Focus on hip stability
4. Progress to full bird dog

**Against Wall:**
1. Set up with feet touching wall
2. Provides feedback if feet move
3. Helps maintain form

### Making It Harder

**Extended Hold:**
1. Hold extended position for 15-30 seconds
2. Builds endurance
3. Greater stability challenge

**Slow Motion:**
1. Take 5-6 seconds to extend
2. Take 5-6 seconds to return
3. Significantly harder on core

**With Pause:**
1. Extend arm and leg
2. Bring elbow and knee together under body
3. Extend again
4. Adds coordination challenge

**On Forearms:**
1. Lower to forearms instead of hands
2. Reduces base of support
3. Greater core demand

**With Resistance Band:**
1. Band around wrists or ankles
2. Adds resistance to movement
3. Strengthening component

## Programming

### For Beginners / Rehab

- **Sets:** 2
- **Reps:** 8-10 each side
- **Hold:** 5 seconds
- **Frequency:** Daily
- **Start with:** Arm only or leg only if needed

### For Core Strength

- **Sets:** 3
- **Reps:** 10-12 each side
- **Hold:** 10 seconds
- **Frequency:** 4-5 times per week

### In a Routine

**Best paired with:**
- Cat-cow (as warm-up)
- Dead bug
- Glute bridges
- Planks (as progression)

**Sample core sequence:**
1. Cat-cow (10 cycles)
2. **Bird dog (10 each side)**
3. Dead bug (10 each side)
4. Glute bridge (15 reps)

## Special Considerations

### With Lower Back Pain

Bird dog is often recommended for lower back pain:
- Start with single-limb versions
- Focus on maintaining neutral spine
- Stop if pain increases
- May be part of PT program

### During Pregnancy

Good exercise throughout pregnancy:
- May need to widen knees as belly grows
- Focus on stability, not intensity
- Stop if any discomfort
- Great for maintaining core function

### Postpartum

One of the first core exercises often recommended:
- Begin with gentle version around 2-4 weeks
- Focus on reconnecting with core
- Pair with breathing exercises
- Progress gradually

### With Wrist Issues

- Come down onto forearms
- Use fists instead of flat hands
- Try on elevated surface
- Wrist wraps for support

## Bird Dog vs. Other Exercises

### Bird Dog vs. Plank

Both are core stability exercises, but:
- Bird dog: Lower load, teaches coordination
- Plank: Higher load, static hold
- Bird dog often better for beginners or back pain
- Can progress from bird dog to plank

### Bird Dog vs. Dead Bug

- Bird dog: Prone (face down), anti-extension focus
- Dead bug: Supine (face up), anti-extension focus
- Both excellent for core stability
- Complement each other in routine

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many bird dogs should I do?

8-12 per side is a good general range. Focus on quality over quantity. Better to do 8 perfect reps than 20 sloppy ones.

### Why do I wobble so much?

Wobbling indicates your stabilizing muscles are working! It's normal at first. Focus on slowing down and reducing range of motion until you can control the movement.

### Can bird dog help back pain?

Often yes. It's frequently prescribed in physical therapy for lower back pain because it strengthens stabilizing muscles without high loads on the spine.

### Should I feel this in my back?

You may feel your back muscles working—that's normal. You should NOT feel pain or strain. If you do, check your form or try an easier version.
