# Using Walkers and Canes: Proper Technique for Seniors

> **The Tool Only Works If Used Right:** Mobility aids help you stay independent—but only when properly fitted and used correctly.

---

## Accepting Mobility Aids

### Not a Sign of Defeat

**The truth:**
- Enables independence
- Prevents falls
- Allows more activity
- Smart choice, not weakness

### Benefits

**Mobility aids provide:**
- Stability
- Confidence
- Safety
- Continued mobility

---

## Canes

### Proper Fit

**Height check:**
- Stand with arm at side
- Cane handle at wrist crease
- When holding, elbow slightly bent (15-20°)
- Too tall or short causes problems

### Which Hand

**Usually:**
- Opposite side from weak/painful leg
- Provides best support
- Ask provider if unsure

### Walking Technique

**Proper use:**
- Move cane with opposite leg
- Cane moves when weaker leg moves
- Creates tripod support
- Smooth, coordinated motion

### Posture with Cane

**Avoid:**
- Leaning heavily to one side
- Hunching over cane
- Cane too far from body

**Do:**
- Stand tall
- Cane close to body
- Light support, not leaning

### Stairs with Cane

**Going up:**
- Good leg first
- Then bad leg and cane together
- "Good goes to heaven"

**Going down:**
- Cane first
- Then bad leg
- Then good leg
- "Bad goes to hell"

## Walkers

### Types

**Standard walker:**
- Lift and move
- Most stable
- Slower pace

**Rolling walker (rollator):**
- Wheels (2 or 4)
- Faster
- Less lifting needed
- Has brakes

### Proper Height

**Fitting:**
- Stand inside walker
- Arms relaxed at sides
- Handles at wrist level
- Slight elbow bend when holding

### Walking Technique

**Standard walker:**
- Lift and place forward
- Step into walker
- Don't move too far ahead
- Stay close to walker

**Rolling walker:**
- Push forward smoothly
- Walk inside frame
- Use brakes when stopping
- Lock before sitting

### Posture with Walker

**Common mistakes:**
- Hunching over
- Walker too far ahead
- Not using properly

**Correct posture:**
- Stand tall
- Eyes forward
- Walker close
- Normal gait pattern

---

## Choosing the Right Aid

### When Cane Is Enough

**Consider cane if:**
- Minor balance issues
- One-sided weakness
- Some support needed
- More independence desired

### When Walker Is Better

**Consider walker if:**
- More significant balance issues
- Need both-sided support
- Greater stability needed
- Falls history

### Getting Professional Advice

**Consult:**
- Physical therapist
- Doctor
- Proper fitting essential
- Training in use

---

## Safety Considerations

### Regular Checks

**Inspect:**
- Rubber tips (worn?)
- Wheels working (rollator)
- Brakes functioning
- Frame solid

### Maintenance

**Keep safe:**
- Replace worn tips
- Tighten loose parts
- Clean regularly
- Address issues promptly

### Environmental Awareness

**Watch for:**
- Wet floors
- Uneven surfaces
- Obstacles
- Rugs that slip

---

## Common Mistakes

### With Cane

**Problems:**
- Wrong height
- Wrong hand
- Leaning too much
- Not coordinating with steps

### With Walker

**Problems:**
- Too far ahead
- Hunching over
- Not using brakes
- Rushing

## Transitioning to Aids

### Adjustment Period

**Normal to:**
- Feel awkward at first
- Need practice
- Build confidence
- Improve over time

### Training

**Get proper instruction:**
- Physical therapy ideal
- Practice in safe environment
- Learn techniques
- Build skill

## Home Modifications

### With Mobility Aids

**Consider:**
- Clear pathways
- Remove trip hazards
- Accessible placement
- Appropriate seating

### Storage

**Keep accessible:**
- Easy to reach
- Ready when needed
- Not tripping hazard

### Still Active

**You can:**
- Walk outside
- Shop

- Stay active

### Modifications

**May need:**
- Rest breaks
- Closer parking
- Seated rest options
- Pacing yourself

### Changing Needs

**Review if:**
- Balance changes
