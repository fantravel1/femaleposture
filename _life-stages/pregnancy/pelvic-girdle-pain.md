# Pelvic Girdle Pain (SPD) During Pregnancy

> **When Your Pelvis Protests:** Pelvic girdle pain can make walking, turning, and daily activities challenging. Understanding the condition helps you manage it.

---

## What Is Pelvic Girdle Pain?

### Understanding PGP

**Pelvic girdle pain (PGP):**
- Pain in pelvic joints during pregnancy
- Includes symphysis pubis dysfunction (SPD)
- SI joint pain often included
- Affects up to 1 in 5 pregnant women

### Anatomy Involved

**Pelvic joints affected:**
- Pubic symphysis (front)
- Sacroiliac joints (back)
- Connections between pelvis and spine
- All loosened by pregnancy hormones

### Why It Happens

**Contributing factors:**
- Relaxin hormone loosens ligaments
- Asymmetrical pelvic movement
- Increased load from pregnancy
- Previous pelvic issues
- Biomechanical factors

---

## Symptoms

### What You Might Feel

**Common symptoms:**
- Pain over pubic bone
- Pain in groin or inner thighs
- SI joint pain (lower back/buttock)
- Pain with walking or stairs
- Clicking or grinding sensation
- Pain with turning in bed
- Difficulty with single-leg activities

### When It's Worse

**Triggers:**
- Walking (especially long distances)
- Climbing stairs
- Getting in/out of car
- Turning in bed
- Standing on one leg
- Separating legs (getting dressed)

---

## Movement Strategies

### The Cardinal Rule

**Keep knees together:**
- Reduces symphysis stress
- Applies to turning, getting up, moving
- Becomes automatic with practice
- Essential for management

### Getting In/Out of Bed

**Technique:**
1. Keep knees together
2. Roll to side as a unit
3. Swing legs off as you push up
4. Don't twist or separate legs
5. Reverse for getting in

### Getting In/Out of Car

**Technique:**
1. Back up to seat
2. Sit down first
3. Swing legs in together
4. Keep knees as close as possible
5. Reverse for getting out

### Stairs

**Strategies:**
- One step at a time
- Lead with stronger leg going up
- Lead with weaker leg going down
- Use handrails
- Reduce stair climbing when possible

### Walking

**Modifications:**
- Shorter stride length
- Avoid waddling
- Supportive footwear
- Limit distance
- Rest as needed

---

## Positions for Relief

### Sleeping

**What helps:**
- Side-lying with pillow between knees
- Equal support for both legs
- Body pillow for comfort
- Keep legs parallel

**What to avoid:**
- Legs crossing
- One leg forward
- Asymmetrical positions

### Sitting

**Best positions:**
- Knees together
- Equal weight on both sit bones
- Supportive surface
- Feet flat on floor

### Standing

**Strategies:**
- Weight even on both feet
- Avoid leaning on one leg
- Short durations
- Supportive footwear

---

## Exercises and Stretches

### What Helps

**Pelvic stability exercises:**
- Pelvic tilts (gentle)
- Core breathing
- Inner thigh squeezes (gentle)
- Gluteal exercises (bilateral)

**Safe stretches:**
- Cat-cow (gentle)
- Seated hip stretches (symmetrical)
- Gentle back stretches

### What to Avoid

**Can worsen symptoms:**
- Wide-legged positions
- Single-leg exercises
- Deep lunges
- Asymmetrical movements
- High-impact activity
- Adductor stretches (inner thigh)

---

## Support Belts

### Pelvic Support Belts

**May help:**
- Provides external stability
- Worn at or below belly
- Different from belly bands
- Many options available

**How to use:**
- Fitted properly
- Not too tight
- Worn during activity
- Can remove for rest

### Types

**Options:**
- Simple pelvic belt
- SI joint belt
- Pregnancy support with pelvic component
- Try before buying if possible

## Professional Help

### When to Seek Help

**See a provider if:**
- Symptoms are significant
- Affecting daily activities
- Pain is severe
- Not responding to self-care
- Concerned about labor

### Who Can Help

**Options:**
- Women's health physiotherapist (specialist)
- Midwife or OB
- Chiropractor (pregnancy-experienced)
- Osteopath

### Physical Therapy

**PT can provide:**
- Specific exercise prescription
- Manual therapy
- Positioning guidance
- Support belt fitting
- Labor position planning

---

## Daily Life Modifications

### Household Activities

**Adaptations:**
- Sit to dress (no standing on one leg)
- Avoid vacuuming or modify technique
- Ask for help with heavy tasks
- Reduce stair trips
- Sit for tasks when possible

### Work

**May need:**
- Ergonomic modifications
- Reduced walking/standing
- Frequent position changes
- Possible reduced hours

### Exercise

**Safe options:**
- Swimming (legs together kicks)
- Water aerobics
- Stationary cycling (narrow stance)
- Upper body exercises
- Prenatal yoga (modified)

---

## Will It Affect Labor?

### Birth Positions

**Planning ahead:**
- Discuss with provider
- Avoid wide leg positions if possible
- Side-lying option
- Upright with support
- Know your comfortable range

### During Labor

**Considerations:**
- Limit leg separation
- Communicate with birth team
- Supported positions
- May affect epidural positioning

### After Birth

**Usually:**
- Symptoms improve after delivery
- May take weeks to months
- Hormones normalize
- PT can help recovery

---

## Emotional Impact

### This Is Hard

**Acknowledge:**
- PGP is painful and frustrating
- Affects independence
- May impact mood
- Support is important

### Getting Support

**Resources:**
- Partner assistance
- Support groups
- Mental health if needed
- Healthcare team

---

## Recovery After Birth

### What to Expect

**Timeline:**
- Relief often begins after delivery
- Full recovery takes weeks to months
- Ligaments gradually tighten
- Some need postpartum PT

### If It Continues

**Seek help if:**
- Symptoms persist beyond 2-3 months
- Interfering with baby care
- Not improving
- New symptoms develop
