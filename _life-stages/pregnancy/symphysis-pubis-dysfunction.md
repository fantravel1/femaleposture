# Symphysis Pubis Dysfunction (SPD)

> **Pelvic pain during pregnancy can significantly impact mobility and daily life. Understanding symphysis pubis dysfunction helps you manage symptoms and maintain function through pregnancy.**

---

## What Is SPD

Understanding the condition helps management.

### Anatomy

**The symphysis pubis:**
- Joint at front of pelvis
- Connects pubic bones
- Normally stable
- Cartilage and ligaments
- Small amount of movement

### During Pregnancy

**What happens:**
- Relaxin hormone loosens joints
- More movement than normal
- Can become unstable
- Pain results
- Varying severity

---

## Symptoms

### Common Signs

**What you may feel:**
- Pain at pubic bone
- Pain radiating to hips or thighs
- Clicking or grinding sensation
- Difficulty walking
- Pain with certain movements

### Triggers

**What makes it worse:**
- Walking (especially stairs)
- Getting in/out of car
- Turning in bed
- Single-leg activities
- Separating legs

### Severity Range

**Varies widely:**
- Mild discomfort to severe pain
- Mildly limiting to disabling
- Individual experience varies
- Can fluctuate
- Take it seriously

---

## Why It Happens

### Hormonal Factors

**Relaxin:**
- Loosens ligaments
- Prepares for birth
- Affects all joints
- Some more sensitive
- Normal but problematic

### Biomechanical Factors

**Contributing:**
- Prior pelvic injury
- Muscle imbalances
- Hypermobility
- Heavy baby
- Multiple pregnancy

---

## Posture and Movement

### Standing

**Modifications:**
- Weight even on both feet
- Avoid one-leg standing
- Core engagement
- Reduce pelvic strain
- Support when needed

### Walking

**Adjustments:**
- Smaller steps
- Keep legs closer together
- Slower pace
- Flat shoes
- Limit distance

### Stairs

**Challenging:**
- One step at a time
- Both feet on each step
- Use railing
- Limit when possible
- Take your time

### Sitting

**Better positioning:**
- Knees together
- Feet flat
- Support behind you
- Avoid crossing legs
- Comfortable positions

---

## Getting In and Out

### Bed

**Technique:**
- Keep knees together
- Roll as a unit
- Use arms to push up
- No leg separation
- Smooth movements

### Car

**Getting in:**
- Sit first, facing out
- Swing legs together
- Keep knees together
- Reverse to exit
- Avoid straddling

### Chairs

**Rising:**
- Feet together
- Use arms
- Don't separate legs
- Controlled movement
- Slow and steady

---

## Treatment Options

### Physical Therapy

**Highly recommended:**
- Specialized pregnancy PT
- Pelvic girdle focus
- Strengthening exercises
- Movement strategies
- Very effective

### Support Belts

**Pelvic belts:**
- Stabilize pelvis
- Reduce movement
- Different from belly bands
- Proper fit important
- Can help significantly

### Other Treatments

**Additional options:**
- Ice or heat
- Swimming/water exercise
- Gentle massage
- Rest
- Activity modification

---

## Daily Life Modifications

### Household

**Adjustments:**
- Sit to dress
- No vacuuming if triggers
- Limit carrying
- Ask for help
- Modify tasks

### Sleep

**Nighttime:**
- Pillow between knees
- Silk or satin sheets (easier turning)
- Roll correctly
- Multiple position options
- Comfort strategies

### Work

**Accommodations:**
- Limit walking
- Frequent position changes
- Ergonomic setup
- May need modified duties
- Discuss with employer

---

## Exercise

### What Helps

**Safe movement:**
- Swimming
- Aqua aerobics
- Gentle strengthening
- Pelvic stability exercises
- Provider-approved

### What to Avoid

**Problematic activities:**
- High impact
- Wide leg movements
- Single-leg exercises
- Aggressive stretching
- Activities causing pain

---

## Preparing for Birth

### Considerations

**Discuss with provider:**
- Birthing positions
- Epidural considerations
- Leg positioning during delivery
- SPD-friendly positions
- Birth plan modifications

### After Birth

**Recovery:**
- Usually improves
- Gradual resolution
- May take weeks to months
- Continued care important
- Physical therapy helpful

---

## When It's Severe

### Seeking Help

**Don't suffer:**
- Mobility aids if needed
- Occupational therapy
- Workplace accommodations
- Home modifications
- Take it seriously

### Assistive Devices

**May be needed:**
- Crutches
- Walking stick
- Wheelchair for distance
- Not failure
- Practical solutions
