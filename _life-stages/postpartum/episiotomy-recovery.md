# Episiotomy and Tear Recovery Posture

> **Healing from perineal trauma requires finding comfortable positions for feeding, resting, and daily activities while protecting your recovery.**

---

## Understanding Perineal Healing

Whether you had an episiotomy or experienced tearing during delivery, your body needs time and proper positioning to heal well.

### What's Healing

**Tissue types recovering:**
- Skin and mucous membrane
- Muscle tissue of perineum
- Connective tissue
- Blood vessels and nerves
- Sutures or stitches

### Recovery Timeline

**General healing progression:**
- Days 1-3: Most swelling and discomfort
- Days 4-7: Swelling decreases
- Weeks 1-2: Sutures dissolving
- Weeks 2-4: Significant healing progress
- 4-6 weeks: Most women feeling improved
- Full healing: 2-3 months

## Comfortable Sitting Positions

### Modified Sitting

**Reduce pressure on perineum:**
- Sit on one hip, shifted to side
- Use firm pillow or donut cushion
- Avoid soft, sinking surfaces
- Keep hips higher than knees
- Change positions frequently

### Ring or Donut Cushions

**Proper use:**
- Place with opening centered
- Use on firm surface, not soft
- Inflate to comfortable level if air-filled
- Move it with you between seats
- Cover with clean cloth daily

### Feeding Positions

**Breastfeeding comfort:**
- Side-lying in bed reduces sitting
- Football hold doesn't require crossing legs
- Reclined positions take pressure off
- Use nursing pillow for support
- Avoid sitting cross-legged

## Movement and Daily Activities

### Getting Up and Down

**Protect perineum:**
- Roll to side before sitting up
- Use arms to push up
- Squeeze glutes before standing
- Stand slowly to prevent dizziness
- Lower gently using arm support

### Walking Tips

**Early movement:**
- Take short, slow walks
- Keep legs close together
- Wear supportive underwear
- Ice after walking if needed
- Gradually increase distance

### Stair Navigation

**Reduce strain:**
- Take one step at a time
- Keep legs close together
- Use handrail for support
- Limit stair trips initially
- Consider staying on one floor

---

## Sleeping Positions

### Optimal Rest Positions

**Comfortable for healing:**
- Side-lying with pillow between knees
- Pillow supporting upper leg
- Avoid lying directly on back
- No pressure on perineum
- Change sides periodically

### Getting In and Out of Bed

**Safe transitions:**
- Roll to side first
- Push up with arms
- Swing legs down together
- Reverse to lie down
- Keep knees together

---

## Bathroom Comfort

### Toilet Posture

**Reduce strain:**
- Sit fully, don't hover
- Lean forward slightly, elbows on knees
- Relax pelvic floor
- Don't strain or push
- Use stool softeners as prescribed

### Cleaning and Care

**Gentle hygiene:**
- Use peri bottle for cleaning
- Pat dry, don't wipe
- Front to back always
- Avoid rough toilet paper
- Apply medications as directed

### Sitz Bath Posture

**Healing soaks:**
- Sit in warm (not hot) water
- Keep hips and perineum submerged
- Lean back slightly for comfort
- 10-15 minutes, 2-3 times daily
- Pat dry completely after

## Early Pelvic Floor Care

### What to Avoid

**While healing:**
- Don't squeeze or do Kegels yet
- No internal exercises
- No tampons or cups
- Avoid heavy lifting
- No deep squats

### When to Start

**After getting clearance:**
- Wait for provider approval
- Usually 4-6 weeks post-delivery
- Start with very gentle contractions
- Build slowly over weeks
- Consider pelvic floor therapy

### Gentle Awareness

**Without active exercise:**
- Notice pelvic floor area
- Practice relaxed breathing
- Let muscles rest and heal
- Avoid clenching or guarding
- Gentle walks help blood flow

---

## Pain Management Positions

### Ice Application

**Reducing swelling:**
- Use ice pack wrapped in cloth
- Apply 10-15 minutes
- While lying on side
- Several times per day early on
- Never directly on skin

### Heat Application

**After initial swelling reduces:**
- Warm sitz bath
- Heating pad on low near area
- Can improve blood flow
- Helps with muscle tension
- Usually after first week

### Comfortable Rest

**Positions for pain relief:**
- Lying on side with pillow support
- Semi-reclined with legs elevated
- Avoid prolonged sitting
- Change positions frequently
- Rest whenever possible

## Partner and Support

### Getting Help

**Accept assistance with:**
- Handing you the baby
- Helping with position changes
- Bringing supplies to you
- Taking baby between feedings
- Managing household tasks

### Communication

**What partners should know:**
- Healing takes time
- Comfort is priority
- Patience with intimacy
- Help without being asked
- Emotional support matters

---

## Signs of Concern

### Normal Healing

**Expected symptoms:**
- Soreness and tenderness
- Mild swelling first few days
- Stitches feeling tight
- Gradual improvement daily
- Minor discomfort with sitting

### When to Call Provider

**Warning signs:**
- Increasing pain after first days
- Foul-smelling discharge
- Opening of wound
- Fever or chills
- Severe swelling or redness
- Inability to urinate

---

## Long-Term Recovery

### Weeks 2-6

**Continued healing:**
- Gradually more comfortable sitting
- Walking distances increasing
- Less need for cushions
- Start gentle pelvic floor work
- Continued perineal massage

### Beyond 6 Weeks

**Full recovery:**
- Return to most normal activities
- Pelvic floor therapy if needed
- Address any ongoing concerns
- Intimacy when comfortable
- Continued self-care
