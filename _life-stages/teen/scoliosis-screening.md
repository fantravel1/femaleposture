# Scoliosis in Teen Girls: What You Need to Know

> **The Facts:** Scoliosis affects 2-3% of adolescents, but girls are 7x more likely to develop curves severe enough to need treatment. Early detection makes a real difference.

## Key Takeaways

- Scoliosis develops most often during growth spurts (ages 10-15)
- Girls are much more likely to have curves that progress
- Screening is quick and can catch issues early
- Most cases are mild and just need monitoring
- Treatment options have improved significantly

---

## What Is Scoliosis?

Scoliosis is a sideways curvature of the spine that also involves rotation. Instead of running straight, the spine curves in an "S" or "C" shape when viewed from behind.

### Types That Affect Teens

**Idiopathic scoliosis (most common):**
- "Idiopathic" means cause is unknown
- Develops during growth
- Has genetic component

**Congenital scoliosis:**
- Present from birth
- Due to vertebrae that didn't form properly
- Usually diagnosed earlier

**Neuromuscular scoliosis:**
- Related to conditions like cerebral palsy
- Due to muscle weakness or imbalance
- Requires specialized management

---

## Why Girls Are More Affected

### The Numbers

- Mild curves occur equally in boys and girls
- Moderate to severe curves are 7-10x more common in girls
- Girls' curves are more likely to progress

### Possible Reasons

- Hormonal differences
- Differences in growth patterns
- Earlier puberty onset
- Possibly genetic factors on X chromosome

## Signs to Watch For

### Visible Signs

- One shoulder appears higher than the other
- One shoulder blade sticks out more
- Waist looks uneven (one side more curved)
- One hip appears higher or more prominent
- Body seems to lean to one side
- Head not centered over pelvis

### When Bending Forward

The "Adam's forward bend test" often reveals scoliosis:

- One side of ribcage appears higher when bending forward
- Back isn't symmetrical when bent over
- This rotation is a key scoliosis indicator

### Other Clues

- Clothes fit unevenly
- Bra straps fall differently
- Back pain (though many teens with scoliosis don't have pain)
- Fatigue with prolonged sitting or standing

## Self-Screening at Home

### What You Need

- Mirror (or smartphone camera)
- Someone to help observe
- Wearing minimal clothing (sports bra, shorts)

### The Forward Bend Test

1. Stand straight with arms at sides
2. Put palms together
3. Bend forward at waist, letting arms hang
4. Keep knees straight
5. Have someone look at your back from behind

**Looking for:**
- Is one side of your back higher than the other?
- Does one rib cage stick out more?
- Is there asymmetry?

### The Shoulder/Hip Check

**Standing straight:**
- Are shoulders level?
- Is one shoulder blade more prominent?
- Are hips level?
- Does waist curve in equally on both sides?

**Important:** This is not a diagnosis. If you notice asymmetry, see a healthcare provider for proper evaluation.

## Professional Screening and Diagnosis

### School Screenings

Many schools conduct scoliosis screenings around ages 10-14. If your school offers this:
- Participate (it's quick and valuable)
- Ask for results to share with your doctor
- Don't skip it even if you feel fine

### Medical Evaluation

If screening suggests possible scoliosis:

**Physical exam:**
- Detailed observation of posture
- Forward bend test
- Measurement of any asymmetries
- Neurological check

**X-rays (if needed):**
- Standing spine X-ray
- Measures curve angle (Cobb angle)
- Determines curve pattern

### Understanding Curve Measurements

- **10-25 degrees:** Mild - usually just monitoring
- **25-40 degrees:** Moderate - may need bracing
- **40+ degrees:** Severe - may need surgery discussion

## Treatment Options

### Observation (Mild Curves)

For curves under 25 degrees that aren't progressing:
- Regular check-ups (every 4-6 months during growth)
- X-rays periodically to monitor
- May include exercises

### Bracing (Moderate Curves)

**When it's recommended:**
- Curves 25-40 degrees
- Still growing
- Goal: prevent curve from getting worse

**How bracing works:**
- Custom-fitted brace worn under clothes
- Worn for prescribed hours (often 16-23 hours/day)
- Works by applying pressure to guide growth
- Most effective when worn as directed

**Modern braces:**
- Lower profile than old designs
- Can be hidden under clothes
- Various options available
- Technology improving constantly

- It works best when worn consistently
- It doesn't always prevent progression
- Support groups can help

### Physical Therapy and Exercise

**Scoliosis-specific exercise programs:**
- Schroth method
- SEAS (Scientific Exercises Approach to Scoliosis)
- PSSE (Physiotherapeutic Scoliosis-Specific Exercises)

**Goals:**
- Improve core strength
- Increase body awareness
- Potentially slow progression
- Reduce pain if present

**These work best:**
- Combined with bracing when indicated
- With specialized practitioner
- With consistent practice

### Surgery (Severe Curves)

**When considered:**
- Curves over 45-50 degrees
- Curves that keep progressing despite bracing
- Curves causing breathing problems
- Significant pain or function loss

**What it involves:**
- Spinal fusion with instrumentation
- Rods and screws to straighten and stabilize
- Major surgery with significant recovery

**Modern results:**
- Much better than years ago
- Most teens return to activities
- Good long-term outcomes for most

## Living with Scoliosis

### Daily Life

Most teens with scoliosis can:
- Participate in all activities
- Play sports (with guidance for severe curves)
- Exercise normally
- Live without restrictions

### What Helps

- Stay active and strong
- Maintain healthy weight
- Practice good posture awareness
- Use ergonomic supports when sitting long periods
- Connect with others who have scoliosis

### Sports and Activities

**Generally fine for most curves:**
- Swimming (excellent)
- Running
- Dance
- Most team sports
- Yoga (with modifications for severe curves)

**May need discussion for severe curves:**
- Contact sports
- Gymnastics
- Sports with repeated hyperextension

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Will my scoliosis get worse?

It depends on several factors: curve size, remaining growth, curve pattern. Most mild curves don't progress significantly. Regular monitoring catches any changes.

### Can I play sports?

Most teens with scoliosis can participate in any sport. For moderate to severe curves, discuss specific activities with your doctor.

### Does bracing really work?

Research shows bracing can prevent curve progression in many cases, but it only works if worn as prescribed. It's a commitment, but it can help avoid surgery.

### Will I need surgery?

Most teens with scoliosis don't need surgery. Only curves that are severe or keep progressing despite bracing typically require surgical intervention.

### Is scoliosis painful?

Many teens with scoliosis have no pain. Some experience back pain or muscle fatigue. Pain management is part of treatment if needed.

### Can exercises cure scoliosis?

Exercise can't cure scoliosis (make the curve disappear), but specific programs can help with strength, function, and potentially slowing progression.

### Will this affect having children someday?

Most women with scoliosis have normal pregnancies. Very severe curves may need special monitoring. Discuss with your doctor when relevant.
