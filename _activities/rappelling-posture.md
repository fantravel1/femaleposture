# Rappelling Posture

Rappelling requires specific body positioning for controlled descent. Proper posture ensures safety.

## Basic Position

### Stance

**On the wall:**
- Feet shoulder-width
- Flat against wall
- Knees slightly bent
- Weight in harness
- Stable position

### Body Angle

**L-shape:**
- Upper body upright
- Legs perpendicular to wall
- Butt sits in harness
- Weight back
- Controlled position

## Rope Management

### Brake Hand

**Control hand:**
- Never let go
- Thumb down grip
- Controls speed
- Life-critical position
- Constant awareness

### Guide Hand

**Upper hand:**
- Guides rope
- Light grip
- Not for braking
- Helps balance
- Assist only

## Descent

### Controlled Speed

**Moving down:**
- Smooth descent
- Walk down wall
- Controlled pace
- Brake hand controls
- Not too fast

### Feet Movement

**Walking:**
- Small steps
- Flat foot contact
- Secure placement
- Deliberate movement
- Trust the system

## Safety

### System Check

**Before descending:**
- All equipment checked
- Harness secure
- Rope threaded correctly
- Backup if appropriate
- Safety first

### Communication

**With belayer/partner:**
- Clear commands
- Verbal confirmation
- Safe practices
- Team awareness
- Standard calls

## Environment

### Terrain Awareness

**On the rock:**
- Watch for loose rock
- Edge awareness
- Rope protection
- Environmental factors
- Safe descent

Good rappelling posture enables controlled, safe rope descent.
